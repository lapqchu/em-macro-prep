# The Markets Exercise — Fast Synthesis Prep

**What this is for.** The shorter of the two exercises. You'll be handed *some material* — you don't know the format in advance — given ~30 minutes to digest it, then asked to present on it for ~10 minutes. There's "nothing specific to prepare" except to *keep abreast of general market developments in Global and EM macro*.

**What it actually tests.** This is not a knowledge test. It's a **fast-synthesis** test:
- Can you walk into unfamiliar material and find the story quickly?
- Can you form a *view* under time pressure, not just describe what you see?
- Can you structure 10 minutes of speaking with no notes-prep time?
- Do you have enough standing macro context that nothing in the material is alien?

The single failure mode: spending 10 minutes *describing* the material instead of *interpreting* it. Description is what a junior does. Interpretation + a view is what they're paying for.

---

## Part A — The 30-Minute Digestion Process

Hard-coded. Don't improvise the process on the day — only improvise the content.

### Minutes 0–5: Survey (don't read deeply yet)

Flip through everything. Build a map:
- What am I looking at? (chart pack? data tables? a research note? a news compilation? a single asset's history? a country dump?)
- What asset classes / countries / time horizons are covered?
- How many distinct "things" are here? (Usually 3–6 exhibits.)
- Is there an obvious intended focus, or is it deliberately broad?

Write at the top of your page: *"This is [type]. It covers [scope]. There appear to be [N] main exhibits."*

### Minutes 5–15: Find the story

Now read properly, but hunting for **2–3 standout things**:
- What's the biggest *move*? (Largest % change, biggest surprise vs expectation, sharpest trend break.)
- What's *anomalous*? (Something that doesn't fit the usual pattern — a divergence, a correlation breaking, a level that looks wrong.)
- What *changed*? (A trend that reversed, a regime that shifted, a new data point that breaks the prior narrative.)
- What's *conspicuously absent or stable*? (Sometimes the story is what *didn't* move.)

Circle 2–3 things. These become your presentation's spine. Resist the urge to cover everything — 3 well-interpreted observations beat 8 described ones.

### Minutes 15–25: Build the view

For each of your 2–3 circled items, push past "what" to "so what":

1. **Observation** — what you see (one sentence, with the number).
2. **Interpretation** — why it's happening / what it means (the causal chain).
3. **Implication** — what it means for markets / policy / a trade.

Then synthesise: **what is the single overall message?** If someone asked "what's the one thing I should take from this material," what would you say? That sentence is your opener.

Then: **if you had risk on, what would you do?** Even if not explicitly asked — have the trade ready. They will likely ask.

### Minutes 25–30: Structure the talk

Write down, in order:
- Your **one-sentence conclusion** (you'll open with this).
- Your **3 supporting points** (observation → interpretation → implication for each).
- Your **risks / what would change your mind** (1–2 items).
- Your **closing line** (restate view + trade).

Do NOT write a script. Write this skeleton. You'll speak from it.

## Part B — The 10-Minute Presentation Structure

| Segment | Time | Content |
|---|---|---|
| **Opener** | 0:00–0:30 | Conclusion-first. *"The key story in this material is X. My read is Y. If I were running risk I'd Z."* |
| **Point 1** | 0:30–3:00 | Observation → interpretation → implication. Strongest point first. |
| **Point 2** | 3:00–5:30 | Same structure. |
| **Point 3** | 5:30–7:30 | Same structure. |
| **Risks** | 7:30–8:30 | "What would change my mind: [A], [B]." |
| **Close** | 8:30–9:00 | Restate the view + the trade in one sentence. |
| **Buffer** | 9:00–10:00 | Leave room — they'll interrupt with questions anyway. |

### Rules

- **Open with the conclusion.** Same rule as Part 2. Never build up to it.
- **Every observation gets a number.** "EM FX sold off" is weak. "EM FX is down 2.5% on the trade-weighted index over the period, led by a 4% drop in [CCY]" is strong.
- **Always reach the "so what."** If you state a fact and stop, you've failed the test. Fact → meaning → implication, every time.
- **Have a trade.** Even if not asked. "If I were positioned, I'd [trade] because [reason], with [risk]."
- **It's fine to say what you'd want to check.** "I'd want to see positioning data to confirm, but my prior is..." — shows process, not ignorance.
- **Expect interruption.** Senior PMs interrupt. Don't be thrown — answer the question, then return to your structure.

## Part C — Templates by Info-Dump Type

You don't know the format in advance. Here's how to handle each likely type.

### Type 1 — A cross-asset dashboard (rates, FX, equities, credit, commodities)

Story is usually a **divergence** or a **regime read**.
- Lead with: "The cross-asset picture is telling [a consistent / a conflicting] story."
- Look for: risk-on vs risk-off coherence; rates vs FX consistency; what's the outlier.
- Trade angle: which asset is mispriced relative to the others.

### Type 2 — A single country's data dump

This is essentially a mini Part 2. Use the **5-concept logic tree** but compressed.
- Lead with: where the country is in its cycle + what the CB should do.
- Look for: inflation trajectory, growth, the policy rate vs Taylor, FX.
- Trade angle: the rate curve, if shown.

### Type 3 — A single asset's price history / chart

Story is the **trend, the break, or the level**.
- Lead with: "This asset is at [level]; the notable feature is [trend / break / divergence]."
- Look for: what drove the move, whether it's sustainable, what's priced.
- Trade angle: continuation vs reversal, and why.

### Type 4 — A news / headline compilation

Story is the **theme** connecting the headlines.
- Lead with: "The connecting thread across these is [theme]."
- Look for: what's market-moving vs noise; what's already priced; what's underappreciated.
- Trade angle: the cleanest expression of the theme.

### Type 5 — A research note / strategist piece

They want your **critical read**, not a summary.
- Lead with: "The author's core call is X; I [agree / partly agree / disagree] because Y."
- Look for: the load-bearing assumption; what happens if it's wrong; what they've underweighted.
- Trade angle: how you'd express it — or why you wouldn't.

### Type 6 — Raw data tables (no charts)

Story is hidden in the numbers — you have to compute the read.
- Lead with the biggest delta you find.
- Look for: surprises vs consensus, trend changes, anything you can quickly turn into a ratio or a change.
- Trade angle: what the data implies for the next print / next policy move.

## Part D — "Keep Abreast" — The Standing Macro Checklist

Walk in able to answer all of these *cold*. Update via Bloomberg the morning of.

### DM central banks — where in the cycle?

- **Fed**: current funds rate, last move, market-priced path, key tension (growth vs inflation).
- **ECB**: deposit rate, cutting/holding, growth backdrop.
- **BoE**: bank rate, the UK-specific inflation persistence issue.
- **BoJ**: where in normalisation, FX/intervention dynamics.

### EM central banks — the big ones

- **Banxico, BCB, BCCh, BanRep** (LatAm) — easing cycles, real rates.
- **CNB, NBP, MNB** (CE3) — who's cut, who's lagging.
- **SARB, RBI, BI, BoK, BoT** — the major Asia/EMEA stories.
- **TCMB, CBE** — the unorthodox / FX-managed cases.

### Key levels (know these to one decimal)

- UST 2y, 10y; Bund 10y; JGB 10y
- DXY; EURUSD; USDJPY; key EMFX (USDMXN, USDBRL, USDZAR, USDCNH, USDTRY)
- S&P 500, VIX
- Brent, gold, copper

### The 3–4 dominant macro themes right now

You should be able to name the live debates — e.g. trade/tariff policy, growth divergence DM vs EM, commodity cycle, fiscal sustainability / term premium, the Fed path. For each: what's consensus, what's contested, what's the risk.

> **Note:** This checklist is the *structure* of what to know. The current-state *content* (May 2026 levels and specifics) you fill in from Bloomberg the morning-of, and we can build a researched current-markets briefing over the weekend. Knowing the structure means nothing in the material will feel alien.

## Part E — Drills

### Drill 1 — The newspaper drill (do daily until the day)

Each morning, open the Bloomberg markets screen or the FT markets page. Give yourself **3 minutes**. Then close it and say out loud, in 60 seconds:
- The one big story
- Why it's happening
- What it means for a trade

### Drill 2 — The chart-pack drill

Take any bank's daily EM or global macro chart pack (you get these at the desk). Give yourself **15 minutes** to digest, then present out loud for **5 minutes** using the Part B structure. Record yourself if you can. Listen back: did you describe, or did you interpret?

### Drill 3 — The "so what" forcing drill

Take any 5 market facts from today. For each, force the chain: fact → interpretation → implication → trade. If you can't complete the chain, you don't understand the fact well enough — go find out why it moved.

### Drill 4 — The cold-open drill

Have someone hand you any single chart or data table you haven't seen. 5 minutes to digest. Then 3 minutes presenting conclusion-first. The point is to get comfortable with the *cold start*.

## Part F — What to Say and Not Say

**Say:**
- "The key story here is..." (conclusion-first)
- "This is up/down [number]; what's notable is..."
- "My read is... — I'd want to confirm with [X] but my prior is..."
- "If I were running risk, I'd [trade] because [reason]; the risk is [Y]."
- "I'd push back on that — here's why..." (when challenged, if you have grounds)

**Don't say:**
- "This chart shows..." then just describe it. (Description, not interpretation.)
- "There's a lot going on here." (You were given 30 minutes — find the story.)
- "I'm not sure what they want me to focus on." (You decide. That's the test.)
- "I don't really have a view." (You must.)
- Covering all 6 exhibits at equal depth. (Pick 3. Go deep.)

---

## Part G — The 60-Second Pre-Walk-In Checklist

Before the markets exercise:
1. The process is fixed: 5 survey / 10 find-the-story / 10 build-the-view / 5 structure.
2. The presentation is: conclusion → 3 points (obs/interp/implication) → risks → close.
3. Every observation gets a number. Every observation reaches a "so what."
4. Have a trade, even if not asked.
5. Description = junior. Interpretation + view = the seat.

---

*End. The muscle this builds — fast synthesis under pressure — is also exactly what a buy-side macro seat demands daily. Drill it.*
